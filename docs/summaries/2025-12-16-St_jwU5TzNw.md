# 關於《世紀帝國II》未來的隱憂：職業選手對「戰力膨脹」與單一玩法的深度剖析

## 重點摘要
職業選手Sattoo擔心，為了推銷DLC，開發商不斷推出過於強大的新文明獨特單位，導致遊戲玩法「單一維度化」。影片主講人對此觀點表示高度贊同，認為這種「戰力膨脹」扼殺了遊戲的策略深度與精妙平衡，並呼籲玩家社群向開發團隊發聲，以挽救遊戲的核心價值。

## 故事大綱
- 開場：主講人介紹他將觀看並評論頂尖法國選手Sattoo的一部影片，該影片標題為「我對《世紀帝國II》的未來感到擔憂」。
- 中段：Sattoo在影片中論證，開發商為了銷量，刻意將DLC新單位設計得過於強大(OP)。這迫使玩家只能使用這些特定單位，從而破壞了遊戲經典的「剪刀石頭布」克制體系，讓策略變得單一。主講人對此深表認同，指出這種「戰力膨脹」(Power Creep)導致遊戲對戰變得「雪球化」，一旦某方取得優勢便難以逆轉。他唯一的輕微歧見在於，他認為這更多是出於開發團隊遊戲理解不足，而非純粹的商業貪婪。
- 結尾：主講人進一步強化了Sattoo的擔憂，批評開發團隊從《神話世紀》或《世紀帝國IV》等相對不成功的遊戲中借鑒機制。他最後總結，玩家社群必須集體發聲，提供反饋，才能引導遊戲回到重視策略平衡的正確方向。

## 關鍵見解
1.  **戰力膨脹 (Power Creep)**：新的DLC不斷引入越來越強的獨特單位，使得舊的通用單位變得過時，並持續推高遊戲的整體單位強度，破壞了原有的平衡。
2.  **單一維度策略 (One-Dimensional Strategy)**：擁有強大獨特單位的文明變得極其可預測，玩家為了獲勝，被迫圍繞該單位制定戰術，扼殺了戰略多樣性。
3.  **核心平衡的侵蝕 (Erosion of Core Balance)**：遊戲從一個講求單位克制、戰術多變的深度策略遊戲，逐漸轉變為比拼誰能更快地爆出OP單位的「執行力」遊戲。
4.  **開發者的方向疑慮 (Concerns about Developer Direction)**：職業選手們普遍認為，開發團隊優先考慮的是如何讓新內容「酷炫」以吸引購買，而非維護遊戲長期競技生態的健康與平衡。
5.  **社群的責任 (The Community's Responsibility)**：兩位選手都認為，玩家社群不能再保持沉默，必須積極表達意見，才能影響開發決策，防止遊戲失去其賴以成功的核心魅力。

## 精彩時刻
- Sattoo的核心論點：「在一個策略遊戲裡，如果策略不再重要，那就只剩下執行力了。」
- 主講人對於借鑒機制的憤慨：「我們為什麼要從那些失敗的遊戲裡搬東西，來放進這個屹立了25年的偉大遊戲裡？」
- 職業選手圈甚至在討論是否需要制定「君子協定」，來禁止在比賽中使用某些破壞平衡的遊戲機制。
- 主講人精準總結了問題所在：「你要嘛就玩那些OP的獨特單位，不然你的文明就感覺什麼都不是。」

# Concerns for the Future of Age of Empires II: A Pro Player's Take on Power Creep & One-Dimensional Gameplay

## TL;DR
Pro player Sattoo expresses concern that new DLCs are making Age of Empires II "one-dimensional" by introducing overpowered unique units to drive sales. The narrator reacts, largely agreeing that this "power creep" dumbs down strategy and harms the game's delicate balance, and he urges the community to voice their concerns to the developers.

## Story Flow
- **Beginning:** The narrator introduces a video by top French player Sattoo, titled "I'm concerned about the future of A[o]E2," setting the stage for his reaction and commentary.
- **Middle:** Sattoo argues that developers intentionally make new DLC units overpowered (OP) to sell content. This forces players into "one-dimensional" strategies, breaking the game's core rock-paper-scissors balance. The narrator strongly agrees, highlighting how this "power creep" leads to "snowbally" gameplay where comebacks are nearly impossible. His only minor disagreement is on developer intent, suggesting it's more about their lack of high-level game knowledge than pure corporate greed.
- **End:** The narrator amplifies Sattoo's fears, criticizing the developers for borrowing ideas from less successful games like Age of Mythology and AoE4. He concludes with a call to action for the player community to provide feedback to save the game's strategic depth.

## Key Insights
1.  **Power Creep:** New DLCs introduce increasingly powerful unique units, making older, generic units obsolete and continuously raising the game's overall power level, which erodes the established balance.
2.  **One-Dimensional Strategy:** Civilizations with strong unique units become predictable, as players are forced to use those units to be competitive, which stifles strategic diversity.
3.  **Erosion of Core Balance:** The game is shifting from one of deep strategy based on unit counters to a game of execution, where success is determined by who can mass the overpowered unit faster.
4.  **Concerns about Developer Direction:** The pro players feel that developers are prioritizing "exciting" new content to sell DLCs over maintaining the long-term health and intricate balance of the competitive scene.
5.  **The Community's Responsibility:** Both players conclude that the community must voice its concerns to influence the game's direction and prevent it from losing its core, successful identity.

## Notable Moments
- Sattoo's core critique: "It's not about strategy, it's about execution then in a strategy game."
- The narrator's frustration with borrowed ideas: "Why are we taking ideas from failed games and bringing it into the one game that lasted 25 years?"
- The revelation that pro players are considering a "gentleman's agreement" to ban certain broken mechanics in tournaments.
- The narrator's succinct summary of the problem: "You have to go unique stuff or else your civ does not feel good."
